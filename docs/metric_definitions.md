# Metric Definitions

This document defines the key commercial metrics used in the FMCG Promotional ROI Case Study.

The purpose of these metrics is to evaluate whether promotional campaigns generated profitable incremental growth, rather than only increasing sales volume.

---

## 1. Baseline Units

**Formula**

`Baseline Units = Expected units sold without promotion`

**Business meaning**

Baseline units represent the expected sales volume if no promotion had occurred. This is the reference point used to measure whether a promotion created incremental demand.

---

## 2. Actual Units

**Formula**

`Actual Units = Observed units sold`

**Business meaning**

Actual units represent the real sales volume during the promotional or non-promotional week.

---

## 3. Uplift Units

**Formula**

`Uplift Units = Actual Units - Baseline Units`

**Business meaning**

Uplift units measure the additional volume generated above expected baseline sales.

A positive uplift indicates that actual sales exceeded expected baseline sales.

---

## 4. Uplift %

**Formula**

`Uplift % = Uplift Units / Baseline Units`

**Business meaning**

Uplift percentage shows how strongly sales volume responded compared with the expected baseline.

This is useful for comparing promotions across products, categories, regions, and accounts with different sales volumes.

---

## 5. Baseline Revenue

**Formula**

`Baseline Revenue = Baseline Units × Regular Price`

**Business meaning**

Baseline revenue estimates the revenue that would have been generated without the promotion.

---

## 6. Actual Revenue

**Formula**

`Actual Revenue = Actual Units × Selling Price`

**Business meaning**

Actual revenue measures the revenue generated during the analysis period.

For promotional rows, selling price is usually the promotional price.
For non-promotional rows, selling price is usually the regular price.

---

## 7. Incremental Revenue from Uplift

**Formula**

`Incremental Revenue from Uplift = max(Uplift Units, 0) × Selling Price`

**Business meaning**

Incremental revenue from uplift measures the revenue generated by additional units sold above baseline.

This metric focuses on the revenue created by incremental sales volume.

---

## 8. Revenue Impact vs Baseline

**Formula**

`Revenue Impact vs Baseline = Actual Revenue - Baseline Revenue`

**Business meaning**

Revenue impact compares actual revenue against expected baseline revenue.

This can be positive or negative depending on whether the promotion generated enough extra volume to offset the lower promotional price.

---

## 9. Baseline Gross Profit

**Formula**

`Baseline Gross Profit = Baseline Units × (Regular Price - Unit Cost)`

**Business meaning**

Baseline gross profit estimates the profit that would have been generated without the promotion.

This is important because the promotion should be judged against the profit that the business expected to earn anyway.

---

## 10. Gross Profit Before Promo Cost

**Formula**

`Gross Profit Before Promo Cost = Actual Units × (Selling Price - Unit Cost)`

**Business meaning**

This measures the gross profit generated during the promotional period before deducting promotional investment.

It captures the effect of both higher sales volume and lower margin from discounting.

---

## 11. Incremental Gross Profit Before Cost

**Formula**

`Incremental Gross Profit Before Cost = Gross Profit Before Promo Cost - Baseline Gross Profit`

**Business meaning**

This measures the gross profit impact of the promotion before promotional costs are included.

A promotion may generate additional sales but still produce weak incremental gross profit if the discount is too deep.

---

## 12. Promo Cost

**Formula**

`Promo Cost = Fixed promotional cost + variable promotional funding`

**Business meaning**

Promo cost represents the investment required to run the promotion.

Examples include:

* Catalogue funding
* Display support
* Retailer funding
* Variable discount support
* Campaign execution cost

---

## 13. Incremental Profit After Promo Cost

**Formula**

`Incremental Profit After Promo Cost = Incremental Gross Profit Before Cost - Promo Cost`

**Business meaning**

This is one of the most important commercial metrics in the project.

It measures whether the promotion generated profit after accounting for margin impact and promotional investment.

A promotion can have high uplift but negative incremental profit if discount depth and promo cost are too high.

---

## 14. Promo ROI

**Formula**

`Promo ROI = Incremental Profit After Promo Cost / Promo Cost`

**Business meaning**

Promo ROI measures how much net incremental profit was generated for each dollar of promotional spend.

Example interpretation:

* ROI greater than 0 means the promotion generated positive incremental profit.
* ROI less than 0 means the promotion lost money after promotional cost.
* Higher ROI indicates better promotional investment efficiency.

---

## 15. Baseline Gross Margin %

**Formula**

`Baseline Gross Margin % = (Regular Price - Unit Cost) / Regular Price`

**Business meaning**

Baseline gross margin percentage shows the normal margin rate before promotion.

This provides the reference point for understanding how much margin is affected by discounting.

---

## 16. Actual Gross Margin %

**Formula**

`Actual Gross Margin % = (Selling Price - Unit Cost) / Selling Price`

**Business meaning**

Actual gross margin percentage shows the margin rate during the sales period.

For promotional rows, this usually decreases because the selling price is lower.

---

## 17. Margin Impact

**Formula**

`Margin Impact pp = Actual Gross Margin % - Baseline Gross Margin %`

**Business meaning**

Margin impact shows how much the promotion changed gross margin in percentage points.

This helps identify promotions where sales increased but margin quality declined.

---

## 18. Rate of Sale

**Formula**

`Rate of Sale = Actual Units / Store Count`

**Business meaning**

Rate of sale measures units sold per store per week.

This is useful for comparing accounts or regions with different numbers of stores.

For example, one region may sell more total units because it has more stores, but another region may have a stronger rate of sale per store.

---

## 19. Cannibalisation Rate Estimate

**Formula**

`Cannibalisation Rate Estimate = Estimated percentage of uplift potentially taken from other products in the same category`

**Business meaning**

Cannibalisation risk estimates whether promotional uplift may have come from shoppers switching away from other products in the same category, rather than creating true category growth.

This is especially relevant in FMCG categories where similar products compete closely with each other.

---

## 20. Cannibalised Units Estimate

**Formula**

`Cannibalised Units Estimate = Uplift Units × Cannibalisation Rate Estimate`

**Business meaning**

Cannibalised units estimate how many uplift units may have been transferred from other products rather than generated as true incremental demand.

---

## 21. Cannibalisation Risk

**Values**

| Risk Level | Interpretation                                                        |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Low        | Promotion likely generated mostly incremental demand                  |
| Medium     | Some category switching risk exists                                   |
| High       | Promotion may have shifted volume from other products in the category |

**Business meaning**

Cannibalisation risk helps account managers and category teams decide whether a promotion should be repeated, adjusted, or reviewed more carefully.

---

## 22. Recommendation Logic

Promotions are classified into commercial action groups.

| Recommendation        | Interpretation                                                               |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Repeat                | Strong ROI, positive incremental profit, and manageable cannibalisation risk |
| Targeted repeat       | Positive performance in selected products, regions, or accounts              |
| Adjust depth/mechanic | Strong uplift but weak profit due to discount depth or promotional cost      |
| Review                | Mixed performance requiring additional commercial context                    |
| Discontinue           | Negative ROI or insufficient commercial return                               |

---

## Key Commercial Principle

A promotion should not be judged by sales uplift alone.

A strong promotion should deliver:

* Incremental sales volume
* Positive or acceptable gross margin impact
* Positive incremental profit after promotional cost
* Positive promotional ROI
* Manageable cannibalisation risk
* A clear commercial action for the sales or account team

The main question is not only whether the promotion sold more units, but whether it generated profitable incremental growth.
